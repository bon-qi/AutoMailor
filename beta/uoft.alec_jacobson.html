I stand against Application Fees. <<Details (updated)>>

Graduate school application fees are put to good use funding administrative efforts required to run graduate admissions. To applicants with sufficient wealth, they are a “1-inch-tall barrier”. To others, they are a significant or even unachievable investment. Application fees create discrimination based on economic standing.

University of Toronto is committed to eliminating discrimination and the School of Graduate Studies is committed to diversity. As these institutions turn their missions into practice, I hope to see the application fees removed in the future. I encourage anyone reading this to write an email to the Associate Chair of Graduate Studies in our department, Graeme Hirst, at gradchair@cs.toronto.edu to show your support for eliminating graduate application fees. Even a brief note is appreciated.

Until these are removed at the departmental level, I will take a very small step. Inspired by Mahdi Cheraghchi, I will personally cover the application fees for a small number (~10) of applicants, from anywhere in the world, applying the MSc or PhD programs in computer science at University of Toronto, who for any reason needed assistance. While I’m hopeful that our Department of Computer Science and the School of Graduate Studies may take steps to remove application fees or create a formal application waiver program, it remains to be determined what changes will happen for this upcoming season of applications. Please return to this site for details in the coming weeks.

To make a request:

  1. send an email to jacobson@cs.toronto.edu with the subject [application fee waivers],
  2. briefly describe your situation and why you need the waiver, include any application materials relevant to your background and track record.

Please be patient. It is likely that I will need to coordinate with selected applicants to arrange to use my personal credit card in the application system (we do not have a waiver codes system).

Describing your situation and needs can be extra trauma or burden that goes against these goals. I sincerely apologize if I am not able to cover your request. I will do my best to reply in a timely manner. If I receive more requests than I can afford, I will regretfully need to prioritize the requests and turn down applicants with justifiable needs and great application materials.

Finally, I am committed to helping dismantle this barrier more uniformly. Please share any stories on how application fees create or have created barriers for you or students you know.

Frequently Asked Questions

These are some questions that I receive fairly regularly in cold-call emails.

Will you hire me as a PhD Student?

Maybe! The first step is to apply directly to the graduate program of the Department of Computer Science or Department of Mathematics. Our regular deadline is in December for students applying to start in the following Fall. Our program is very competitive. Most students put months of effort into their application package. Your research interests should match or harmonize with mine (see publications above for an idea). You should not only mention this in your application (and mention me by name), but also write about how and why we’re a great match. If you’d like to chat about research or life at dgp as a graduate student, feel free to contact me.

Will you hire me as a postdoc?

Maybe! Postdoc positions are very limited. If I’m not already aware of your research then chances are much smaller. In any case, contact me to begin the conversation. Have a one-to-two year research agenda prepared.

In nearly all circumstances, I will ask you to apply for a postdoc fellowship (and I will help you apply). Please review relevant fellowships, including those specifically for Canadian permanent residences (if applicable) or “mobility” fellowships from your home country/country of PhD.

I’m an undergrad from University of Toronto, could I work as your research

assistant?

Maybe! Undergraduate research assistantships are limited and competitive. You should be a proficient programmer and feel confident in Calculus and Linear Algebra. If you haven’t already, sign up for the undergraduate Computer Graphics course and my graduate-level Geometry Processing course (and probably also David Levin’s Computational Fabrication and Kyros Kutulakus’s Foundations of Computational Vision courses). Good performance in these courses will put you at a great advantage. Also, start attending the dgp graphics reading group and the dgp graphics seminar talks. If you’re hoping to work with me over the summer, reach out to me around January/February. Give an indication of what research of mine most interests you and what sort of way you could imagine yourself contributing (in practice, we will try to find a current project to match you with, but this information will help).

I’m an undergrad from X â  Unversity of Toronto, could I work as a your

research assistant?

Maybe! External undergraduate research assistantships are even more limited than internal ones. If you can secure or apply for funding from your home institution or country then you will be at a great advantage. If you do not already have a funding source, then before contacting me investigate whether fellowships for exchange programs etc. exist in your institution/country. If there are none, then consider applying to one of the summer undergraduate research fellowship programs at University of Toronto (e.g., at the Fields Institute). Finally, if you still cannot find funding, contact me with an application including a statement of purpose, faculty references, (unofficial) transcripts, and resume.
